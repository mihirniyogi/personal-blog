### what's this series about?

I've recently gotten into Greek Mythology and am reading Mythos by Stephen Fry. Rather than a contiguous flow, Mythos is a collection of short, distinct (but related) stories featuring the birth and origins of gods, demigods and mortals alike. I keep forgetting the stories, however; there are simply too many! Too many names and too many shenanigans by Zeus. Thus, after each story or chapter, I aim to write up a short recap/review of my thoughts. The hope is, after thinking about the chapter and seeing how it relates to life, it would help me keep the lessons in mind and also make it easier to remember the stories themselves. 

### Sisyphus

Though he is but a mere mortal, there are multiple stories of him defying the gods, and death himself, TWICE. What's surprising, I think, is how well defiance and cunning served him throughout his longer-than-usual life and also the _extent_ of his cunning to get his way. His trickeries include but are not limited to:

- Sisyphus exposes Zeus for abducting Aegina, extorting a reward from her father, a river god Asopos.
- To punish him for violating Xenia (sacred hospitality), Zeus sends Thanatos, the god of death. 
- Sisyphus tricks Thanatos and imprisons him, halting _death_ entirely.
- Hermes frees him, setting Thanatos upon Sisyphus, but he cheats death in a roundabout way by manipulating his burial rites and tricking Persephone. 
- He is ultimately condemned to forever pushing the boulder to the top (and believing that it is the way) to escape hell, only for it to roll back down just before he reaches the top. A "Sisyphean task" is therefore a task that requires constant effort but is never-ending.  

![Painting of Sisyphus by Titian](Punishment_sisyph.jpg "25%: Painting of Sisyphus by Titian (Public Domain, via Wikimedia Commons)")

It's impressive how far Sisyphus' wits and sheer audacity carries him. But alas, one can't escape the consequences of one's actions, only delay them. How come only mortals have to face these consequences?  

### Hubris - Niobe

The story of Niobe is one that depicts her tragic downfall as a result of _hubris_. She had fourteen children and boasted endlessly about their royal lineage; about how much better she was than the Titaness Leto who only had the twins Apollo and Artemis. In return, the twins each shot dead half of Niobe's children. Her grief knew no bounds - she wept uncontrollably, so much so that even after she was turned to stone, her tears pushed themselves beyond the rocks. To this day, Türkiye's Mount Spil still shows the outlines of a female face in a formation whose name translates to Weeping Rock. _No mortal is above the gods_. 

![Picture of Niobe's Rock](niobe_weeping_rock.jpg "25%: Picture of Niobe's Rock by Carole Raddato (Public Domain, via Wikimedia Commons)")

### Hubris - Marsyas

The downfall from hubris continues! Marsyas the satyr picked up an instrument that Athena had invented, cursed and thrown down to Earth. He played the _aulos_ so well that he drunkenly challenged Apollo, god of the lyre. After Apollo outplayed Marsyas, as impartially judged by the Muses, he decided a befitting punishment was to _tear the literal skin off his body;_ the title "The Flaying of Marsyas" says it all. _No mortal is above the gods_. And the gods are violent as hell.
